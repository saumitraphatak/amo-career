Beam Steering: The Two-Mirror Walk

most important skill

A single mirror has two degrees of freedom (tip and tilt) but you need to control four parameters: x and y position and angle in two planes. Two mirrors give you all four. The two-iris (near/far) walking technique converges the beam iteratively to any target.

Two-mirror walk — M1 controls angle at near iris · M2 controls position at far iris · iterate until both are centred

The geometry

Place two irises along the beam path: one near (just after M2) and one far. The near iris controls which mirror affects position, the far iris controls angle.

  1. Close near iris tight. Use M2 to steer beam through near iris.
  2. Open near iris. Close far iris. Use M1 to steer beam through far iris.
  3. Repeat: M2 for near, M1 for far. Each cycle converges closer.
  4. After ~3–4 iterations both irises should pass simultaneously.
  5. Open irises, verify beam hits target (e.g. fiber coupler, AOM aperture).
Why it works: M1 is far from the near iris, so it mostly rotates the beam around the near iris position. M2 is close to the near iris, so it mostly translates the beam at the far iris. The decoupling is never perfect — hence iterating.
Common mistake: Using M1 to fix the near iris and M2 to fix the far iris. This is backwards and diverges. Always: M2 → near, M1 → far.
Counterpropagating alignment: For aligning into a cavity or fiber, send a reference beam backwards from the output. A back-propagating beam through the same path instantly shows you the axis to match. Use IR viewer cards or a beam profiler.

Beam Height Adjustment

periscope · post height

All beams on an optical table should travel at a consistent height (typically 3" or 4" in most AMO labs — agree with your group and never mix). Changing height while keeping the horizontal position and direction fixed requires a periscope: two mirrors at 45°, one sending the beam up, one returning it horizontally at the new height.

Periscope — M1 (45° up) raises beam · M2 (45° down) returns it horizontal at target height · beam direction unchanged
  1. Set M1 at 45° to the beam, deflecting it vertically upward.
  2. Adjust M1 post height so beam exits at the right intermediate height.
  3. Set M2 at 45° facing downward, sending beam back to horizontal at target height.
  4. Walk the two periscope mirrors using near/far irises to ensure both horizontal planes are parallel.
  5. Verify output height with a beam profiler or ruler on a post.
Checking height uniformly: Place a standard post (e.g. 3" Thorlabs post with a pinhole at the tip) at multiple points along the beam path. If the beam clips the same height at all points, you're level. A beam that's slightly tilted will miss by millimetres over a metre.
Watch for: A periscope that's not perfectly vertical will introduce a horizontal walk as the beam goes up. Use a mirror mount with fine horizontal adjustment on M1 to correct.

Checking Beam Collimation

divergence · shear plate

A collimated Gaussian beam has a flat wavefront and a waist at infinity — its size changes only due to diffraction over the Rayleigh range. In practice, a beam from a fiber output or laser diode needs a collimating lens adjusted to make the beam as parallel as possible over your working distance.

Collimation test — measuring beam width at two distances. If w₁ ≈ w₂, beam is collimated. Diverging/converging beams show clear size change.

Method 1: Two-point size measurement

Measure the beam diameter at two points separated by ≥ 0.5 m using a beam profiler or burn paper. If sizes match → collimated. If beam grows → diverging (move collimating lens farther from source). If beam shrinks → converging (move lens closer).

  1. Mark beam size at position A (near collimating lens).
  2. Mark beam size at position B (0.5–1 m away).
  3. Compare: if B > A → diverging. If B < A → converging.
  4. Translate collimating lens along beam axis, repeat until A ≈ B.

Method 2: Shear plate interferometer

A glass wedge creates two laterally sheared copies of the beam that interfere. Collimated: straight, evenly-spaced fringes. Converging/diverging: curved fringes. Tilt the plate until fringes are straight.

Autocollimator trick: Retroreflect the beam off a flat mirror placed far away. If the return beam perfectly overlaps the output, you're collimated. Any offset → residual divergence.

Lens Centering & Finding Focus

on-axis · avoid coma

A beam that enters a lens off-axis or at an angle experiences coma and astigmatism. For high-NA objectives or tight-focus tweezers, even a fraction of a mm off-axis degrades your PSF and coupling. Centering the beam on the lens axis is non-negotiable.

Lens centering — walk beam through lens. On-axis: beam exits straight. Off-axis: beam kicks sideways. Use lateral lens translation to null the kick.

Centering on the lens

Mount the lens on an XY translation stage. Place an iris far downstream. Translate the lens in X and Y until the beam position at the iris does not change as you move the lens — this is the on-axis condition. Equivalently: the beam retroreflects straight back when you place a flat mirror behind the lens.

  1. Coarse: visually centre beam on lens aperture using a white card.
  2. Place an iris 0.5–1 m downstream, close to beam size.
  3. Translate lens laterally in X. If beam moves at iris → not centred.
  4. Adjust X until translating the lens doesn't move the beam at iris.
  5. Repeat for Y axis.

Finding the focal point

Translate lens along beam axis. The focus is where beam size is minimum — measured with a beam profiler or by observing the scatter from a cover slip. For high-NA objectives, use the back-reflection from a glass surface: the reflection is collimated when the surface is exactly at focus.

Aspheric lenses: These have a designated input side (usually the flat face). Reversing an aspheric will dramatically worsen your focus. Check the spec sheet. For achromats, the "crown" glass side should face the collimated beam.

The 4F Relay System

spatial filtering · beam expansion · SLM imaging

A 4F system (two lenses separated by f₁+f₂, object at distance f₁ before lens 1, image at distance f₂ after lens 2) performs a perfect relay with magnification −f₂/f₁. It's ubiquitous in AMO labs: SLM imaging, beam expansion, spatial filtering, and combining beams at a shared focal plane.

4F relay — L1 Fourier-transforms the input. Back focal plane = Fourier plane (spatial frequencies). L2 inverse-transforms back to real space. Magnification = f₂/f₁.

Alignment procedure

  1. Collimate input beam (verify with two-point method).
  2. Place L1 at distance f₁ from object/SLM plane. Use a ruler — this spacing sets the Fourier plane.
  3. Place an iris at distance f₁ behind L1 (the Fourier plane). The beam should focus here.
  4. Walk the beam through L1 using two mirrors so it passes on-axis (iris at Fourier plane stays centred when translating L1).
  5. Place L2 at distance f₁+f₂ from L1 (i.e. f₂ behind the Fourier plane).
  6. Centre L2 on the diverging beam using XY stage.
  7. Verify output: collimated beam of diameter d_out = d_in × (f₂/f₁).

Spatial filtering

Place a pinhole at the Fourier plane. Low spatial frequencies (plane wave components) focus near the centre; high frequencies (scatter, diffraction rings) focus further out. The pinhole passes only the central spot, cleaning up the beam.

Pinhole size: For a Gaussian beam with waist w₀ at the Fourier plane, use a pinhole diameter ≈ 2–3 × w₀. Too small → you clip the main beam. Too large → scatter passes through.

SLM imaging: The SLM must be imaged onto the back focal plane of the microscope objective. This requires a 4F between the SLM surface and the objective BFP. The Fourier plane in between is where you place the spatial filter to block undiffracted 0th-order light.

Aligning Two Wavelengths on a Dichroic

dichroic mirror · co-propagation

A dichroic mirror transmits one wavelength and reflects another. In a tweezer experiment, you typically need 780 nm (cooling) and 1064 nm (trap) to be perfectly co-propagating into the objective. Misaligned beams at the objective back aperture create separated foci in the sample plane — fatal for single-atom loading.

Dichroic alignment — λ₁ transmitted, λ₂ reflected. Two separate steering mirrors bring each wavelength to the same axis. Walk λ₁, then walk λ₂ to overlap λ₁.
  1. Align λ₁ (transmitted) through the dichroic using its own steering mirrors, to target irises on the far side.
  2. Block λ₁. Align λ₂ (reflected) off the dichroic using its own steering mirrors, hitting the same irises.
  3. Unblock λ₁. The two beams should now overlap on the irises. Fine-tune λ₂ steering mirrors to maximise overlap.
  4. Check far-field: place a lens after the dichroic. Both beams should focus to the same point.
  5. Iterate near/far iris walking separately for each wavelength until far-field foci coincide.
Chromatic shift: Even perfect co-propagation at the dichroic doesn't guarantee co-focal spots — the objective has chromatic aberration. For λ₁ = 780 nm and λ₂ = 1064 nm, the axial shift can be 1–5 µm depending on objective type. Use a water-immersion objective with NIR correction or compensate with a lens in the 1064 nm path.
Using a camera to verify overlap: Image both beams with a single CCD (sensitive to both wavelengths). At the focus of a lens, two perfectly co-propagating beams will be concentric. Any tilt = elliptical overlap. Any offset = positional misalignment.
Wedge in dichroic: Many dichroic beamsplitters have a small wedge angle to prevent etalon effects. This means the reflected beam exits at a slightly different angle than expected. Always verify the reflected angle experimentally, don't trust the nominal 45°.

Single-Mode Fiber Coupling

mode matching · coupling efficiency

Coupling into a single-mode fiber (SMF) requires matching the input beam waist to the fiber mode field diameter (MFD ≈ 5–6 µm for 780 nm SM fiber) and aligning both position and angle to within a fraction of a micron. A good coupling is 70–85%; above 90% requires a clean beam and careful mode matching. Below 50% — something is wrong with the beam shape or alignment.

SMF coupling — input beam waist must match fiber MFD at the fiber tip. 5-axis coupler (x,y,z,tip,tilt). Maximise by walking mirrors then fine-tuning coupler x/y.

Mode matching

Choose coupling lens focal length f such that the beam waist at focus equals the fiber MFD/2:

w₀ = λf/(πw_in) ≈ MFD/2

For 780 nm into a 5 µm MFD fiber with a 1.5 mm input beam: f ≈ 11 mm. Use an aspheric lens for best results.

  1. Calculate target f for your beam size and fiber MFD.
  2. Walk beam to fiber coupler using two mirrors and two irises (far iris = fiber input).
  3. Watch output power on power meter. Once you see any coupling, it's a good sign.
  4. Optimise coupler x/y and z (focus distance). Use a 5-axis coupler (Thorlabs KT110).
  5. Fine-tune input beam position on coupling lens with steering mirrors.
  6. Iterate coupler + mirrors until coupling is maximised. Expected: 70–85% for a clean beam.
Why coupling is low (<50%):
• Beam is astigmatic (check with profiler)
• Beam size mismatch (wrong lens or diverging input)
• Fiber end face dirty (clean with IPA + wipe)
• Beam clipping somewhere upstream (check all apertures)
• AOM alignment off (beam not hitting center of AOM)
Using backreflection: Send a reference beam backwards from the fiber output. It will propagate back through the coupler and retrace the path to your laser. Align your input beam to overlap this reference — then forward coupling is maximised immediately. This is 10× faster than hunting in forward direction alone.

Table Discipline & Hardware Choices

clamping · forks · posts · stability

The best alignment in the world is wasted if your mounts are unstable. Optical tables drift, vibrate, and creep — but good clamping practices reduce this by orders of magnitude. This section covers the non-glamorous but critical art of mounting hardware correctly.

🍴
Fork Clamps

Always use fork clamps (Thorlabs CF125) to secure post holders. A free-standing post will drift by hundreds of µm overnight from temperature changes. The fork clamp bolt should be tightened to ~10–15 in-oz — snug, not stripped.

📏
Post Length

Keep posts as short as possible while clearing the table. Long posts = resonant at low frequencies (~100 Hz) = coupling mechanical noise into your beam. Max 4" post for a beam at 3" height. Use a pedestal post base if needed.

⚖️
Mount Choice

Polaris and Suprema mounts (Thorlabs) have far lower drift than standard KM100 mirror mounts. For anything in a beam path you'll never want to touch again (e.g. after the fiber), use a Polaris. Save KM100 for temporary setups.

🌡️
Thermal Stability

Cover beam paths with black anodised aluminium covers when not actively aligning. Temperature drafts from HVAC shift mirrors by µrad/°C. A 1° change in room temp can move your AOM coupling by 1–5% over an hour.

🔲
Beam Blocks

Block all stray beams and backreflections immediately. Even a weak backreflection into a laser can cause mode hopping. Use beam dumps (anodised aluminium wedge), not white cards taped to posts.

📝
Documentation

Photograph the beam path with a ruler visible, draw a schematic in your lab notebook the same day. When you break your alignment at 11pm three months later you will thank yourself.

🔧
Tightening Order

When securing a post: (1) hand-tighten the post set screw, (2) place fork clamp, (3) tighten fork bolt, (4) final-tighten set screw. Tightening set screw first without the fork will cause the post to shift when you add the clamp later.

🪟
Window & Window Wedges

Any glass surface (viewport, waveplate, PBS) creates an etalon and a parasitic reflection. Tilt flat optics by ~2–3° to deflect backreflections away from the beam path. Wedge windows to prevent etalon fringes.

The "jiggle test": After aligning, gently push on each post/mount while watching the beam on a card. Any mount that makes the beam move when lightly tapped needs to be re-clamped or replaced. In a vibration-sensitive experiment (tweezer, cavity), every mount that fails this test is a noise source.

Alignment Checklist

before you call it done

Before starting any alignment

  • Laser safety goggles on. Know which wavelengths are present.
  • Laser power at minimum or mode-locked off for initial coarse work.
  • All unnecessary beams blocked. Clean the fiber end faces.
  • IR viewer card charged. Beam profiler powered up.
  • Know your target beam height, size, and direction before touching anything.

During alignment

  • Two-mirror walk for every major steering step. Never try to align with one mirror.
  • Check beam size after each lens — confirm no unexpected clipping.
  • Verify collimation at 2 points before sending into any sensitive component.
  • Centre beam on every lens using translation stage + downstream iris.
  • Document each step (photo + schematic sketch).

After alignment

  • Jiggle-test every mount. Re-clamp anything that moves.
  • Add fork clamps to all unsecured posts.
  • Block all stray beams with proper beam dumps.
  • Record coupling efficiencies, beam sizes, and powers in lab notebook.
  • Let beam path thermalize 1 hour, then re-check coupling.
  • Cover beam path if not actively using it.

Red flags — re-align if…

  • Fiber coupling drops >5% over a day without changing anything.
  • MOT loading rate decreases unexpectedly.
  • AOM diffraction efficiency drops (beam drifted off crystal).
  • Tweezer loading probability falls below expected for your NA.
  • You hear a "pop" or "click" from any mount (something slipped).
The golden rule: Never adjust a mirror "just a little" without knowing why the beam drifted. Find the root cause first (thermal drift, loose mount, mode-hop). Random mirror tweaking creates correlated downstream problems that are much harder to debug.
